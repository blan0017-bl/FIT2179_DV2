The Shaking
Continent

Australia sits deep inside a tectonic plate, far from the boundaries that rattle Japan or New Zealand. Yet between 2005 and 2026, more than 5,000 earthquakes were recorded beneath it. Here is where the continent shakes, how hard — and why the risk falls hardest where the fewest people live.

Bryan Landy · 35873191 · FIT2179 · Data Visualisation 2

We tend to picture earthquakes as someone else's problem — a hazard for the Pacific “Ring of Fire”, not for the world's flattest, oldest and most stable continent. Yet Australia is not silent. Over the two decades to 2026, Geoscience Australia logged 5,280 earthquakes of magnitude 2.5 or greater. Most were too faint to notice; a handful cracked walls, closed highways and sent tens of thousands of people into the street. Where they strike — and who actually feels them — is not what most Australians would guess.

01

Where the ground moves

Plot every quake and a hidden geography emerges. The eastern seaboard, where most Australians live, is comparatively quiet. The activity is inland and west: a dense band through south-west Western Australia around Meckering and Kalgoorlie, a cluster along the Flinders Ranges in South Australia, and scattered shocks across the centre. These are intraplate earthquakes — they occur far from any plate boundary, where ancient crust is slowly squeezed. Almost all sit within about 10 kilometres of the surface.

02

How often, and how hard

Two basic questions: how often does Australia shake, and how hard? On the left, the yearly count jumps around but shows no clear upward trend — Australia isn't shaking more than it used to; we are simply recording more of it. On the right, the magnitudes follow a harsh arithmetic: 87% of quakes fall below magnitude 3.5, the point at which people begin to notice. Each step up the scale means roughly ten times fewer events.

03

The biggest quakes run shallow

Plotting every quake by its size and depth reveals a quiet rule of Australian seismicity: almost everything happens in the top 10 kilometres of crust, and the largest shocks are no exception. Shallow quakes pass their energy efficiently to the surface — another reason a magnitude-5 event here can rattle an entire town.

04

The biggest jolts on record

Across two decades, only a handful of continental quakes broke magnitude 6. The strongest were a 6.6 off Broome in 2019 and a 6.1 in the Petermann Ranges in 2016 — powerful, but in the remote north-west and centre, far from any city. The 5.9 near Woods Point in 2021 stands out: smaller, yet right on Melbourne's doorstep. (Australia's single largest tremor, a 7.4 in 2008, struck beneath sub-Antarctic Macquarie Island — geologically Australian, but 1,500 km from the mainland, so it sits outside this map of the continent.)

But size matters out of all proportion. Each step up the magnitude scale releases roughly thirty times more energy, so a few large quakes dominate completely: the single biggest — the 2019 Broome quake — released over half of all the seismic energy of the past two decades, and the ten largest account for four-fifths of it.

05

Counts versus people

Here is the twist. By raw count (left), Western Australia dominates, with South Australia second and the east trailing. But divide by population (right; ABS, September 2025) and the order flips: the Northern Territory — just 266,000 people — tops the list at around 1,800 quakes per million residents, more than thirty times the rate of New South Wales or Victoria. Per person, the emptiest parts of Australia shake the most.

The flip is starker as a change in rank. Western Australia slips from first to second, New South Wales falls from third to fifth, and the Northern Territory leaps from fourth all the way to first.

06

The risk map, per person

The same finding, mapped. The arid centre and west burn bright; the crowded south-east coast stays pale. It is the central irony of Australian seismicity — the ground moves most where almost no one is there to feel it.

07

Who actually feels it

Magnitude isn't everything — what matters to people is whether a quake happens near them. The most-reported quake in the dataset wasn't a giant in the outback but a magnitude-5.9 near Woods Point, Victoria, on 21 September 2021, which shook Melbourne and drew over 43,000 “felt” reports. A modest 3.8 near Sunbury drew nearly 28,000. The quakes Australians feel are the ones beneath the south-eastern cities — not the larger, lonelier shocks out west.

08

The cities closest to the action

So which of the places Australians actually live face the most shaking? Counting earthquakes within 200 km of each capital reveals a final twist: Perth — ringed by the south-west's active faults — tops the list with nearly 400 nearby quakes, ahead of Melbourne. Brisbane, by contrast, sits almost untouched, with barely ten. Each circle is sized by the city's population and shaded by how many quakes occur nearby — so Australia's calmest big city and its most exposed one are, remarkably, both home to millions.
